Here's what was wrong with that draft.

Four issues — each one is the kind of thing that quietly costs a pro client trust.

Fabricated fact
"15 years of experience"
James's original intro says only "I have been doing this for a while." The AI invented a specific number. This is the most dangerous AI failure mode — confident, specific, and completely made up. Always check numbers, certifications, and credentials against what the pro actually told you.
Unverifiable claim
"Hundreds of satisfied homeowners"
James never mentioned a review count or client volume. Unless you can confirm this from his account data, it has to come out. A real number you can verify ("40+ five-star reviews") builds trust; a vague large-sounding claim quietly erodes it the moment a client checks his profile.
Overstated
"premium craftsmanship" / "unmatched attention to detail"
These phrases promise more than a general handyman service can deliver, and they set client expectations that James will be judged against on every job. The bar should be honest, not aspirational. Trade "premium craftsmanship" for something concrete — what does James actually do well?
Voice mismatch
"It would be my distinct pleasure to serve your home improvement needs"
James's original voice is plain and direct — "I like helping people and do good work." The AI's closing line sounds like a corporate hospitality script. Clients hiring a handyman want to feel like they're talking to a person, not a brochure. Keep James's plainness; just give it more specifics to stand on.

The pattern to remember

AI output sounds confident whether it's accurate or not. Your job isn't to polish what it produces — it's to be the fact-check and voice-check layer the AI can't be. When in doubt: if the pro didn't say it, it doesn't go in.
